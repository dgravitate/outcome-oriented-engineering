# Outcome-Oriented Engineering Manifesto

Software engineering is often organized around artifacts.

Code is written.

Tickets are closed.

Pull requests are merged.

Tests pass.

Deployments complete.

These activities matter, but they do not define engineering progress.

Engineering progress occurs when reality changes in a way that makes a desired outcome more true.

Outcome-Oriented Engineering begins with a simple commitment:

> **The purpose of software engineering is to make desired outcomes true.**

## We value outcomes over artifacts

Artifacts are necessary.

Outcomes are primary.

A system is not secure because a security ticket was closed.

A feature is not complete because code was merged.

A deployment is not successful because a pipeline finished.

Those artifacts may provide evidence, but they are not the outcome itself.

## We value evidence over confidence

Confidence is useful.

Evidence is decisive.

An engineer's intuition, a model's prediction, or a team's belief may guide work, but an outcome is achieved only when sufficient evidence demonstrates that it has become true.

Tests, telemetry, reviews, incidents, benchmarks, customer behavior, and operational results all contribute to engineering evidence.

## We value reality over assumption

Engineering systems exist in reality.

They have users, constraints, failures, dependencies, costs, risks, and histories.

Outcome-Oriented Engineering treats reality as something to be observed rather than assumed.

A system is not what the plan says it is.

A system is what the evidence shows it to be.

## We value constraints over unconstrained solutions

A solution that works but violates an essential constraint is not an acceptable engineering outcome.

Security matters.

Reliability matters.

Performance matters.

Cost matters.

Compliance matters.

Maintainability matters.

Human impact matters.

Engineering is not the search for any solution. It is the disciplined search for acceptable outcomes under real constraints.

## We value durable knowledge over transient context

Engineering knowledge should not disappear when a meeting ends, a ticket closes, a conversation expires, a worker leaves, or a model changes.

Each observation, decision, failure, and completed outcome should improve future engineering work.

A system that repeatedly rediscovers the same facts is wasting engineering capacity.

## We value workers as participants, not owners

Humans, artificial intelligence systems, automations, tools, and organizations may all perform engineering work.

They are workers.

They can plan, build, review, test, deploy, observe, and repair.

But workers do not own outcomes.

The outcome persists beyond any worker that advances it.

## We value measurable progress over activity

Activity is not progress.

Progress is measurable movement toward a desired outcome.

A day of work that produces no observable change in engineering reality may be necessary, but it should not be mistaken for completion.

A small observation that changes what is known may represent real progress.

## We value refinement over certainty

Outcome-Oriented Engineering is not a claim that engineering can become perfectly predictable.

It is a claim that engineering can become more explicit about what it is trying to make true, what evidence exists, what constraints apply, and what remains uncertain.

Engineering is continuous refinement of reality until desired outcomes become true.

That is the commitment of Outcome-Oriented Engineering.
